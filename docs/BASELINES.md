# Baseline comparison

> Subset numbers. Generated by `panelcast compare --baselines` on the ~5,000-album
> AOTY subset — the same real splits the model is evaluated on (see
> [`MODEL_CARD.md`](../MODEL_CARD.md) → *Real-data subset re-baseline*). Model rows
> are from the 2026-07-02 re-baseline under the promoted `offset_logit` transform
> (#43). Full-corpus validation is tracked in
> [#15](https://github.com/cupidthatbtc/panelcast/issues/15).

This page states, in the open, **where the hierarchical Bayesian model actually
sits against simple baselines** — because that placement is the honest argument for
what the model is *for*. (`reports/baselines/` is gitignored, so the generated
table is otherwise invisible in the repo.)

## Within-entity temporal holdout — predict an entity's next event (N = 653)

| Model | MAE | RMSE | R² | CRPS | 80% cov | 95% cov | 95% width |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| gradient boosting | **5.41** | **7.76** | **0.486** | **4.01** | 0.770 | 0.899 | 22.7 |
| conformal GBM | 5.59 | 7.90 | 0.468 | 4.09 | 0.822 | 0.946 | 31.4 |
| ridge | 5.62 | 8.00 | 0.455 | 4.22 | 0.873 | 0.962 | 33.8 |
| **panelcast (Bayesian)** | 5.66 | 8.19 | 0.429 | 4.13 | 0.864 | 0.959 | 33.4 |
| last score | 6.10 | 8.88 | 0.328 | 4.71 | 0.900 | 0.971 | 39.4 |
| entity mean | 6.11 | 8.92 | 0.322 | 4.59 | 0.818 | 0.925 | 29.8 |
| global mean | 7.23 | 10.91 | −0.015 | 5.55 | 0.890 | 0.956 | 43.3 |

Read without flattery:

- **On point accuracy the model is mid-pack.** Gradient boosting (MAE 5.41,
  R² 0.486) and ridge (5.62, 0.455) both beat it (5.66, 0.429). A flexible
  regressor extracts more point signal from these features than the structured
  Bayesian model does. If a point estimate is all you need, use the GBM. (The
  `offset_logit` re-baseline narrowed the R² gap — 0.417 → 0.429 — and its CRPS
  4.13 now edges ridge's 4.22; the MAE tick 5.64 → 5.66 is the Student-t
  robust-loss trade.)
- **It clears the naive entity baselines by a real margin.** R² 0.429 vs the
  per-entity mean's 0.322 and persistence's 0.328 — the hierarchical partial
  pooling does real work over `np.mean`-per-entity (just not more than a GBM).
- **The point-accuracy winner buys it by under-covering.** GBM's intervals are a
  residual-spread afterthought: 80% coverage 0.770 and 95% coverage 0.899 — it is
  over-confident. The model's intervals are a *modeled* quantity and land at
  nominal (0.864 / 0.959). Ridge is the strong all-rounder (good MAE, slightly
  over-covering).

So the model's justification is **not** "wins the leaderboard." It is: calibrated
intervals as a first-class modeled output (not a residual wrapper), an
**interpretable variance decomposition** (how much score variance is between-entity
vs within-entity residual), a generative model you can interrogate (posterior
parameters, PPC), and a structure that ports to a new domain by swapping one
descriptor. Those are uncertainty-quantification deliverables, not point-accuracy
ones — and a user who only needs a point estimate should reach for the GBM.

> That sharper test has now been run
> ([#50](https://github.com/cupidthatbtc/panelcast/issues/50)): **conformal GBM**
> wraps the same GBM in split-conformal calibration (75/25 proper-train/calibration
> split; predictive samples resample held-out signed residuals, so the intervals
> are distribution-free). It keeps most of the GBM's point edge (MAE 5.59 vs 5.41;
> the gap is the 25% of train held out for calibration) and reaches near-nominal
> coverage (95%: 0.946; 80%: 0.822) with *narrower* 95% intervals than the model
> (31.4 vs 33.4). So **calibration alone is cheap** — the model's interval
> quality can be approximated without a likelihood on this split. (Under the
> earlier `identity` baseline the wrapper even edged the model on cold-start
> coverage; the re-baselined model reversed that — 0.956 vs 0.912 at 95%, see
> below.) The honest value proposition for the Bayesian model remains what
> conformal cannot produce: a generative story you can interrogate (posterior
> parameters, PPC) and the entity-vs-residual variance decomposition.

## Cold-start — a brand-new entity with no history (artist-disjoint, N = 799)

| Model | MAE | R² | 80% cov | 95% cov | 95% width |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| **panelcast (Bayesian)** | **7.01** | **0.095** | 0.839 | 0.956 | 42.8 |
| conformal GBM | 7.27 | 0.037 | 0.713 | 0.912 | 31.4 |
| gradient boosting | 7.31 | 0.037 | 0.640 | 0.810 | 21.7 |
| ridge | 7.33 | 0.083 | 0.820 | 0.937 | 33.3 |
| entity mean | 7.69 | −0.001 | 0.743 | 0.900 | 29.1 |
| last score | 7.69 | −0.001 | 0.837 | 0.947 | 38.0 |
| global mean | 7.69 | −0.001 | 0.901 | 0.957 | 44.0 |

The `offset_logit` re-baseline changed this split's story. Under the earlier
`identity` baseline the model sat at MAE 7.19 / R² 0.003 — a statistical tie
with the entity-mean fallback — with over-confident intervals (95% coverage
0.896) that were *narrower* than its within-entity ones. Under the transform it
**leads the table on both MAE and R²** (7.01 / 0.095), its 95% coverage is at
nominal (0.956), mean bias halved (+2.8 → +1.5), and its cold-start intervals
are now appropriately *wider* than the within-entity ones (42.8 vs 33.4) — the
population predictive on the logit scale respects the bounds instead of leaking
mass past them.

Read without flattery, still: R² 0.095 is weak in absolute terms — debut
reception remains close to unpredictable from the available features (the
covariates-only headroom is ≈ 0.083, [`.audit/genre_pooling/`](../.audit/genre_pooling/covariates_only_r2.md)),
and every method's MAE sits within ~0.7 of the global mean's. The conformal
wrapper's guarantee assumes exchangeability, false by construction for
never-seen entities; it degrades accordingly (0.946 → 0.912), though it still
beats the raw GBM's 0.810. A second, group-level pooling tier for this split
ships as `entity_group_pooling` (#41): the paired screening run measures a
further cold-start gain (MAE −0.14, R² +0.034 — essentially the entire
genre-block headroom;
[`.audit/genre_pooling/gate_on_screening.md`](../.audit/genre_pooling/gate_on_screening.md)).
Since 0.6.0 the gate defaults to auto-on (#85: multi-seed screening plus a
publication-scale confirmation) wherever the dataset descriptor defines a
group column; domains without one keep the single pooling tier.

## R² is an average over a steep history gradient

The within-entity R² ≈ 0.43 hides a strong dependence on how much history an entity
has — and it runs *opposite* to the intuition that more history helps the *fit*:

| Train events | n | R² | RMSE | 95% cov |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| 1–2 | 124 | **0.516** | 5.93 | 0.968 |
| 3–5 | 262 | 0.444 | 7.11 | 0.962 |
| 6–10 | 180 | 0.381 | 8.94 | 0.967 |
| 11+ | 87 | 0.334 | 11.64 | 0.920 |

R² is **highest for thin-history entities** (1–2 events → 0.52) and **falls as
catalogs deepen** (11+ → 0.33), while RMSE roughly doubles. Thin-history entities
are *easiest* — they regress tightly to the population mean — whereas prolific
entities have idiosyncratic, higher-variance trajectories that are genuinely harder
to predict in absolute terms. So the headline R² is conditional on the history mix
of the holdout, which is itself conditional on the minimum-history / minimum-ratings
selection filter. Quoting a single 0.43 overstates the model on deep catalogs and
understates it on thin ones — report the gradient.

## Reproduce

```bash
panelcast run --stages splits,features   # if the split/feature artifacts aren't present
panelcast compare --baselines            # writes reports/baselines/ (gitignored)
```

`reports/` is gitignored (large, regenerable), so this page is the curated,
version-controlled copy. The committed snapshot of the model's own evaluation lives
at `.audit/baseline_metrics.json`, regenerated from the current default model
(`user_score_20260702_195732.nc`, the 2026-07-02 `offset_logit` re-baseline).
